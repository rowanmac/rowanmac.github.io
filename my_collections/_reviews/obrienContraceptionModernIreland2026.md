abstract: "A tour de force of feminist history, Laura Kelly\u2019s Contraception and\
  \ Modern Ireland is an intimate exploration of reproductive health and activism\
  \ in the twentieth century. The story begins with the establishment of the Irish\
  \ Free State (1922) and ends with the Health Amendment Act of 1992, which allowed\
  \ adults to acquire contraceptives without a prescription. The twentieth century\
  \ gave rise to a coalition between Irish nationalists \u2014 who associated contraceptives\
  \ with degenerate English morals \u2014 and the papal hierarchy, which adopted an\
  \ increasingly anti-contraceptive stance acrost the twentieth century. Over nine\
  \ chapters, Kelly first tracks the effect of pronatalism on people\u2019s lives,\
  \ and then shows how feminists mounted a successful family planning movement. While\
  \ Kelly makes exhaustive use of archival sources, oral history defines and enlivens\
  \ this book. The testimonies of 116 people (103 born before 1955) yield rich insights\
  \ about people\u2019s emotional lives. Kelly\u2019s actors leap off the page and\
  \ speak directly to the audience, resulting in a personal and memorable read. This\
  \ methodology also prompts reflection on the messiness and ambiguity of people\u2019\
  s subjective self-perceptions."
