abstract: "While surfing in Ireland is a sport on the ascendancy, both in terms of\
  \ recreational and competitive participants, a great deal more work needs to be\
  \ done on the sport\u2019s origin within the country. This is especially the case\
  \ for women\u2019s surfing on the island. Irish women entered surfing from the 1960s\
  \ onwards, with the role of geography, family networks, and equipment access shaping\
  \ participation patterns. Women\u2019s entry mechanisms into surfing was historically\
  \ entrenched in masculine cultures. Irish female surfers have navigated, challenged,\
  \ and reshaped the boundaries of this sport in many ways, delineating how Ireland\u2019\
  s surfing narrative, whilst rooted in its distinct coastal communities, has been\
  \ part of global surfing cultures. Women who successfully entered Irish surfing\
  \ navigated informal barriers, revealing mechanisms of exclusion that operated differently\
  \ from institutional prohibition while demonstrating how Ireland\u2019s peripheral\
  \ position necessitated immediate engagement with global surfing networks."
