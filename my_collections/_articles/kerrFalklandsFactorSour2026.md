abstract: "This article examines the misunderstood role of the Falklands War in reshaping\
  \ Anglo-Irish relations at a critical moment in the early 1980s. Rather than setting\
  \ Anglo-Irish relations on a new trajectory, it argues that the crisis consolidated\
  \ and deepened divisions that had emerged during the 1980\u201381 hunger strikes,\
  \ closing the pathway towards an intergovernmental settlement of the Northern Ireland\
  \ conflict. It shows how Haughey\u2019s Falklands-era diplomacy was not anomalous\
  \ but formed part of a domestic political strategy: he sought to use Anglo-Irish\
  \ relations\u2014and Northern Ireland in particular\u2014as a lever to stabilise\
  \ a precarious minority government and secure a governing mandate. Thatcher interpreted\
  \ the Irish Government\u2019s actions during the crisis as confirmation that Dublin\
  \ was a high-risk partner.1 Haughey\u2019s efforts to assert Ireland\u2019s sovereignty\
  \ and political relevance therefore placed strain on Anglo-Irish relations, while\
  \ reinforcing Thatcher\u2019s instinctive reluctance to pursue far-reaching political\
  \ engagement. It demonstrates that the Falklands War marked a turning point in British\
  \ and Irish thinking about Northern Ireland. By hardening mistrust and legitimising\
  \ a cautious, security-first approach to intergovernmental cooperation, the crisis\
  \ foreclosed the prospect of an \u2018agreed Ireland\u2019 between the two governments\
  \ and helped shape the narrower framework of Anglo-Irish diplomacy that followed."
